LOCATION: The Midnight Continental Express
A private luxury sleeper train traveling overnight from Vienna to Istanbul, December 1923. Eight carriages of polished mahogany and brass, carrying the elite of European society through a snowstorm in the Balkans. The train has been stopped at a remote mountain pass after the body was discovered — no one can leave until the matter is resolved. The dining car still smells of champagne and cigars from the evening's celebration.

THE CRIME: Count Aleksander Dvorak — A wealthy Czech arms dealer, 58, found dead in his private sleeping compartment (Cabin A4) at approximately 1:15 AM. He had been hosting a champagne dinner in the dining car earlier that evening to celebrate a major arms contract. The Count was universally feared but rarely liked — he had ruined competitors, blackmailed associates, and left a trail of broken alliances across Europe.
Cause of death: Stabbed once through the heart with a narrow blade while lying in his berth. Death was near-instantaneous.

THE SUSPECTS:
  • Major Viktor Hessler — Retired Austrian military officer and arms broker: A decorated war veteran turned arms dealer who was the Count's chief business rival. They had been locked in a vicious bidding war over the same Ottoman military contract. Hessler is gruff, proud, and short-tempered, known for making threats he rarely follows through on. He was overheard at dinner saying 'One day that man will push someone too far.'
  • Marguerite Fontaine — French cabaret singer and socialite: A glamorous Parisian performer who was the Count's current mistress. She was his guest of honor at the dinner and wore a diamond necklace he'd gifted her that evening. Marguerite is charming and calculating, adept at reading rooms and managing powerful men. She appeared devoted to the Count publicly.
  • Dr. Emil Kurtz — Swiss physician and the train's on-call doctor: A meticulous, soft-spoken man in his 60s who has served as personal physician to several wealthy European families. He was hired specifically for this journey to attend to the Count's heart condition. Kurtz is precise, clinical, and uncomfortably observant — he notices everything and volunteers nothing.
  • Pyotr Volkov — Russian émigré and the Count's personal valet: A quiet, efficient man in his 40s who has served the Count for eight years. Volkov knows all of the Count's secrets, schedules, and habits. He is deeply loyal on the surface but carries himself with a contained tension. He was responsible for preparing the Count's cabin each evening.
  • Teodora Novak — Young Serbian heiress traveling to Istanbul: A composed, sharp-eyed woman of 26 who boarded the train in Vienna. She presented herself as traveling to Istanbul to settle her recently deceased father's shipping business. She was polite but reserved at dinner, and exchanged only brief pleasantries with the Count. She carries herself with quiet intensity but shows no obvious hostility toward anyone.
  • Inspector Felix Brandt — Off-duty German police inspector: A sharp, sardonic Berlin detective in his 50s who happens to be a passenger on the train, traveling to Istanbul on holiday. He has a professional interest in crime and couldn't resist attending the Count's dinner party when invited. Brandt is observant, opinionated, and can't help analyzing everyone in the room.

OPENING CLUES:
  • The Count was stabbed once through the heart with a narrow blade while lying in his berth. The weapon — an ornate stiletto letter opener from the Count's own writing desk — was left on the cabin floor, wiped clean of prints.
  • The cabin door was found unlatched but not locked. The Count's own key was still in his coat pocket, meaning either he let someone in willingly or someone had a duplicate key.
  • A small smear of dark leather polish was found on the inside door handle — the kind used on military gloves, fine ladies' gloves, or leather medical bags.
  • The Count's brandy decanter had been moved and a second glass was poured — he was drinking with his killer before the attack. The second glass was also wiped clean.
  • A porter reports seeing a figure in dark clothing moving quickly through the corridor between Sleeping Car A and Sleeping Car B at approximately 12:50 AM, but could not determine build, height, or gender. The corridor was dim.
