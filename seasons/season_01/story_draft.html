The Lineup — Season 1 Story Draft

THE TOMB OF AMENHOTEP

Egypt, 1893. The Valley of the Kings.

Setting

A newly discovered tomb deep beneath the Egyptian desert. Lord Reginald Ashworth, a wealthy British aristocrat, has funded a private expedition to explore the burial complex of Pharaoh Amenhotep — a minor king whose tomb was believed lost for three thousand years.

The expedition descended three levels into the earth, passing through corridors covered in hieroglyphs, past false doors and ancient traps, into a vast burial chamber housing a golden sarcophagus. The air is stale. Torchlight flickers against painted walls depicting the afterlife.

Then the sandstorm hit. The entrance shaft filled with sand in minutes. They are sealed inside — no way out until the storm passes and a rescue party can dig through. That could be hours. It could be days.

And then Thomas Burke, the expedition guide, was found dead in the burial chamber. Stabbed in the back with his own excavation knife.

Eight people descended into this tomb. One of them is a killer. No one is leaving until the truth is found.

The Detective

Inspector William Cross — Scotland Yard detective, 48, on loan to the Egyptian Antiquities Service in Cairo. Cross was assigned to accompany the expedition after intelligence suggested someone in the group was connected to the black market antiquities trade. Methodical, sharp-eyed, and deeply skeptical of everyone's stated reasons for being inside a pharaoh's tomb. He didn't want this assignment — he considers tomb exploration reckless — but now he's trapped underground with a dead man and seven suspects.

The Victim

Thomas Burke — Irish expedition guide, 42. A former British Army surveyor who settled in Luxor and became one of the most sought-after tomb guides in Egypt. Rough, hard-drinking, and blunt, but nobody knew the Valley of the Kings better. Burke had a dangerous habit: collecting secrets about his clients and using them as leverage — for money, for favors, or simply for the pleasure of watching powerful people squirm. He was found face-down beside the sarcophagus in the main burial chamber, an excavation knife buried between his shoulder blades. Death was quick.

The 7 Suspects

1. Lord Reginald Ashworth

British aristocrat and expedition funder, 55

A pompous, silver-haired English lord who inherited a fortune and a fascination with ancient Egypt from his late father. Ashworth funded the entire expedition and considers everything inside the tomb his personal property. He treats the other members as employees, regardless of their credentials.

Alibi: Claims he was in the antechamber examining wall carvings with Professor Kessler when Burke was killed. Says he heard nothing unusual — the thick stone walls muffle everything.

Secret: Ashworth is nearly bankrupt. His family fortune is gone, squandered on bad investments and expensive collections. This expedition is a desperate gamble — he needs to find sellable artifacts to avoid financial ruin. If word gets out, his reputation and social standing are destroyed. Burke knew about the debts and had been quietly extorting him for months.

Red herring: Found arguing with Burke an hour before the murder about "what belongs to whom." A servant overheard Ashworth say, "You'll get nothing more from me, Burke. Nothing." His excavation gloves have a small tear — and a matching leather fragment was found near the body.

2. Dr. Colette Beaumont

French archaeologist from the Sorbonne, 38

Brilliant, passionate, and furious about the British plundering of Egyptian heritage. Beaumont is one of the few women in professional archaeology and has fought for every ounce of respect. She joined the expedition officially to document the tomb's historical significance, but she's also working as an informant for the Egyptian Antiquities Service — reporting on whether Ashworth intends to smuggle artifacts.

Alibi: Claims she was in the lower passage sketching hieroglyphs on the second level, alone, with only her lantern. Says she didn't return to the burial chamber until she heard shouting.

Secret: Beaumont has been secretly removing small artifacts from sites for years — not for money, but to donate them to French museums rather than see them "rot in British private collections." She's exactly the kind of smuggler Inspector Cross was sent to find, but she's smuggling for what she considers a noble cause. Burke discovered her hidden stash and threatened to expose her.

Red herring: She was seen entering the burial chamber alone thirty minutes before the body was discovered. Her leather satchel contained wrapping materials and a small crowbar — tools that could be used for theft or worse. She also carries a sharp palette knife for cleaning artifacts.

3. Professor Otto Kessler

German Egyptologist, 62

A quiet, meticulous scholar who has spent thirty years deciphering hieroglyphs. Kessler believes this tomb contains a previously unknown text — a lost chapter of the Book of the Dead — that would be the discovery of his career. He's been translating inscriptions obsessively since they entered the tomb, barely interacting with the others.

Alibi: Claims he was with Lord Ashworth in the antechamber, cross-referencing inscriptions with his notebooks. Says he was so absorbed in his work that he lost track of time and didn't notice Burke leave or anyone else move through the passages.

Secret: Kessler has been falsifying translations for years, publishing papers based on invented hieroglyphic interpretations that made his career. If the real inscriptions in this tomb don't match his published work, his fraud will be exposed. Burke, who spoke some Arabic and had learned to read basic hieroglyphs from years in the Valley, noticed discrepancies and confronted Kessler privately.

Red herring: His notebooks contain pages torn out — recently. If examined, the torn edges match paper fragments found near Burke's body. He also has ink stains on his hands that he claims are from his work, but the ink matches a threatening note found in Burke's pocket.

4. Catalina Vasquez

Spanish journalist and adventurer, 31

A sharp, charismatic reporter writing a serialized account of the expedition for a Madrid newspaper. Vasquez has a gift for getting people to talk and an instinct for being in the right place at the wrong time. She's charming, fearless, and always scribbling in her notebook.

Alibi: Claims she was in the corridor between the antechamber and the burial chamber, writing notes by torchlight. Says she heard footsteps moving quickly past her in the dark — heading toward the burial chamber — but couldn't see who it was.

Secret: Vasquez isn't just a journalist. She was hired by a rival collector in Madrid to steal a specific artifact from the tomb — a golden scarab said to be inside the sarcophagus. She's been paid half up front and needs the scarab to collect the rest. Burke caught her trying to open the sarcophagus alone the previous night and demanded a cut.

Red herring: Her notebook contains detailed sketches of the sarcophagus mechanism — far more detailed than a journalist would need. She was also seen having a heated whispered conversation with Burke in the lower passage earlier that day. A gold coin — her advance payment — was found dropped near the entrance to the burial chamber.

5. Reverend James Whitmore

American missionary from Boston, 50

A tall, earnest man who presents himself as a religious observer, here to "ensure the dead are treated with proper Christian respect." He's uncomfortable with tomb raiding and frequently quotes scripture about disturbing the dead. The others find him preachy and slightly odd — why would a missionary join an archaeological expedition?

Alibi: Claims he was praying in the upper corridor near the sealed entrance, asking God to protect the expedition. Says the storm made terrible sounds and he was focused on prayer until he heard the alarm raised.

Secret: Whitmore is not a real reverend. He's a former confidence man from New York who reinvented himself as a missionary after a fraud conviction. He came to Egypt running from creditors and joined the expedition because he heard rumors of treasure. Burke recognized him from a wanted poster in a Cairo police station and had been blackmailing him since the second day.

Red herring: He was seen leaving the burial chamber area approximately twenty minutes before the body was discovered, walking quickly and looking agitated. His Bible has a hollowed-out center — containing a set of lockpicks and a small derringer pistol. His "missionary credentials" are forged.

6. Yuki Tanaka

Japanese cartographer and artist, 29

Hired to map the tomb's passages and illustrate the findings for publication. Tanaka is quiet, precise, and intensely observant. While others argue and posture, Tanaka watches, sketches, and says little. This makes some of the group uncomfortable — they can't read him, and he seems to notice everything.

Alibi: Claims he was on the second level mapping a newly discovered side passage. Says he was completely alone, working by the light of a single oil lamp, and heard nothing until Vasquez came running down to announce the murder.

Secret: Tanaka is not just a cartographer. He's an agent of the Japanese Imperial government, sent to acquire Egyptian antiquities for Japan's growing museum system. His mission is to photograph everything in the tomb and, if possible, secure diplomatic rights to future excavations. Burke found Tanaka's camera hidden in his equipment — photography in tombs was explicitly forbidden by Ashworth — and threatened to have him expelled from the expedition.

Red herring: His detailed maps show a passage that connects the second level directly to the burial chamber — a passage no one else knew about. He could have traveled to and from the murder scene without being seen in the main corridor. His mapping instruments include a sharp steel compass that could be used as a weapon.

7. Dimitri Volkov

Russian mercenary and expedition security, 40

A broad-shouldered, scarred man hired by Ashworth to protect the expedition from tomb robbers and bandits. Volkov speaks little, drinks less, and keeps his hand near his revolver at all times. He claims this is just another job, but he's been inside Egyptian tombs before — and not always on the right side of the law.

Alibi: Claims he was patrolling the upper level near the sealed entrance, checking the stability of the passage after the sandstorm started. Says the sand collapse kept him occupied and he didn't return to the lower levels until after the body was found.

Secret: Volkov was hired by a Moscow antiquities dealer to steal the contents of the sarcophagus — the same golden scarab that Vasquez is after. He and Vasquez don't know they're competing for the same object. Burke discovered Volkov's true employer when he found a letter written in Russian in Volkov's pack. Burke couldn't read Russian, but he showed the letter to Tanaka, who could — and Tanaka told Burke what it said.

Red herring: His revolver has been recently fired — he claims he shot at a snake in the upper passage, but no snake was found. He has sand on his boots inconsistent with his claimed location. A torn piece of his coat sleeve was found caught on a torch bracket in the corridor outside the burial chamber.

The Killer

Reverend James Whitmore

What Really Happened

Burke cornered Whitmore in the lower passage after dinner, demanding more money — double what he'd been paying. Burke threatened to tell Inspector Cross about the wanted poster and the forged credentials. Whitmore, already broke and desperate, knew that exposure meant prison — not just for the fraud, but for outstanding warrants in three American states.

Whitmore waited until the camp settled. Around 10 PM, he took Burke's own excavation knife from the equipment pile — Burke always left his tools laid out carelessly. He found Burke alone in the burial chamber, examining the sarcophagus by torchlight. Whitmore approached from behind, quoting scripture — "The wages of sin is death" — and drove the knife between Burke's shoulder blades.

He wiped his hands on his dark coat, returned to the upper corridor, and resumed praying as if nothing had happened. The body was discovered forty minutes later when Ashworth sent Volkov to fetch Burke for a meeting.

The Weapon

Burke's own excavation knife — a heavy, broad-bladed tool used for clearing packed earth. Taken from the expedition equipment pile in the antechamber. Left in the body.

The Motive

Self-preservation. Burke's escalating blackmail threatened to expose Whitmore's entire false identity — the fake credentials, the fraud convictions, the outstanding warrants. Whitmore saw no way out except to silence Burke permanently.

Timeline

  • 9:00 PM — Expedition members settle into their assigned areas for the night
  • 9:15 PM — Burke and Whitmore seen arguing quietly in the lower passage (witnessed by Vasquez from the corridor)
  • 9:30 PM — Burke goes to the burial chamber alone to inspect the sarcophagus
  • 9:45 PM — Whitmore takes the excavation knife from the equipment pile in the antechamber
  • 9:50 PM — Whitmore enters the burial chamber, confronts Burke, stabs him
  • 9:55 PM — Whitmore returns to the upper corridor, resumes praying
  • 10:30 PM — Volkov discovers the body when sent to find Burke

Evidence

Opening Clues (Given to all at start)

  1. Burke was stabbed once in the back with his own excavation knife, which was left in the body. The knife was taken from the expedition equipment pile in the antechamber — anyone could have accessed it.
  2. Burke was found beside the sarcophagus in the main burial chamber. His torch was still burning, suggesting he hadn't been dead long.
  3. A crumpled note was found in Burke's coat pocket reading: "This is your last warning. Pay what you owe or face the consequences." The handwriting is disguised — block capitals, no identifiable style.
  4. Faint boot prints in the dust of the burial chamber show two sets of tracks — Burke's heavy work boots and a second, lighter set that approaches from behind and then retreats toward the main corridor.
  5. A torn scrap of dark fabric was found caught on a torch bracket in the corridor just outside the burial chamber, at roughly shoulder height.

Round 4 Evidence Reveal

The torn fabric matches the lining of a dark coat — not expedition-standard gear. Additionally, Inspector Cross's examination of the equipment pile confirms the excavation knife was removed between 9:30 and 10:00 PM. Ashworth and Kessler were together in the antechamber during this window but both claim they didn't notice anyone take the knife. Finally, a second note was found hidden inside Burke's bedroll: a list of names with amounts written beside them — Ashworth: £200, Whitmore: £50, Kessler: £30, Vasquez: £100. A blackmail ledger.

Key Story Dynamics

Why this works for the game: - Every suspect has a real secret Burke was exploiting — creating genuine suspicion for all - The blackmail ledger (Round 4 evidence) gives the detective concrete leads - Multiple suspects have physical evidence against them (Ashworth's gloves, Beaumont's tools, Kessler's torn pages, Whitmore's coat, Volkov's fired gun, Tanaka's secret passage) - The killer (Whitmore) has a strong cover story (praying alone) but physical evidence (coat fabric) and motive (blackmail) that can be uncovered - The setting creates natural isolation — no one can leave, limited light, sound doesn't carry through stone - The Victorian-international cast creates natural personality clashes and cultural tension
